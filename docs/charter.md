# RHDH AI-Augmented Pod — Charter

## The Bet

We believe that a small, focused team equipped with AI tooling can deliver more than the sum of its parts — not just faster, but differently. This Pod is a structured experiment to find out what an AI-augmented engineering team looks like in practice, what it can achieve, and what reusable patterns emerge along the way.

This is a strategic bet, not a reaction to capacity constraints. We want to learn early how AI changes the way product teams operate — before it becomes table stakes.

## What the Pod Is

A focused sub-team within the RHDH engineering organization. The Pod operates across the full RHDH stack: UI plugins, backend, CI/CD, documentation, and operator.

The Pod remains fully integrated with the broader team — same ceremonies, same backlog, same accountability. The difference is *how* the work gets done.

### Composition

| Role | Responsibility |
|---|---|
| **Guidance** | Strategic direction, AI tooling expertise, skill development |
| **Pod Lead** | Day-to-day execution, technical leadership |
| **PO / PM** | Prioritization, blocker removal, stakeholder alignment |
| **Engineer** | Feature delivery, full-stack contributions |

### How It Works

- The Pod pulls tickets collectively from the sprint backlog rather than taking individual assignments.
- AI tooling is used at every level: individual coding assistance, shared skills and agents, and end-to-end autonomous workflows where the human steers and reviews.
- Guidance and Pod Lead sync daily to maintain alignment and iterate on approach.

## What We Want to Prove

**Thesis:** A small AI-augmented team can achieve higher velocity than a comparably-sized traditional team — while simultaneously producing durable assets that accelerate the broader organization.

Two dimensions of output:

1. **Sprint delivery** — Successful completion of sprint and release tickets at or above team baseline velocity.
2. **Reusable assets** — Skills, documentation, automation, and workflows that outlive the pilot and benefit the entire team.

### Measuring Success

Concrete measurement methods will evolve with the experiment. Initial signals to watch:

- Sprint velocity relative to team baseline
- Quality indicators (bug rate, regressions, review turnaround)
- Number and adoption rate of reusable assets produced
- Team feedback on collaboration and knowledge transfer

## AI Integration — Three Layers

**Layer 1: Copilot.** Every Pod member uses AI-assisted coding tools (Claude Code) for day-to-day development — writing code, debugging, reviewing PRs, navigating unfamiliar parts of the codebase.

**Layer 2: Shared Knowledge.** The Pod builds and maintains a shared library of skills, agent configurations, and workflows. These encode team knowledge in executable form — not just documentation, but tools that actively assist.

**Layer 3: Autonomous Workflows.** For well-understood, repeatable tasks, agents take over entire workflows end-to-end: generating PRs, running test suites, updating issue trackers, syncing transcripts. The human role shifts from executing to steering and reviewing.

## Risks

| Risk | Mitigation |
|---|---|
| Adoption drag — new tools slow the team down before they speed it up | Ramp period; early sprints set baselines rather than targets |
| Quantity over quality — AI makes it easy to produce more, not necessarily better | Quality metrics tracked alongside velocity; review standards unchanged (Commandment VI) |
| Knowledge silos — learnings stay locked inside the Pod | Asset creation is an explicit output goal; skills are designed for org-wide use (Commandments II, IV) |
| Over-reliance on tooling — process breaks when tools are unavailable | Core skills remain human-executable; agents augment, they don't replace competence |
| Transparency fatigue — too much data, not enough signal | Regular retrospectives to prune what's measured and shared |

## Timeline

**Initial pilot:** Multiple sprints with end-of-sprint retrospectives to evaluate velocity, quality, and viability.
